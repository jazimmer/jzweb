A Film Review

 
Early in this film elderly caregiver Jang Mi-ja learns that she is coming down with Alzheimer's disease. Given the other obstacles she faces, namely
raising an unruly miscreant of a grandson,
fending off employer sexual advances,
and learning to write poetry,
you might expect emotional fireworks. But Jang Mi-jan's is a gentle person who makes moral decisions you can admire even if you don't quite approve of them.
 
This film caused me to research the concept of blood money. It turns out that paying blood money has a surprising counterpart in American jurisprudence. To understand the similarity think of the trials of O.J. Simpson. First there was a criminal trial which acquitted him of the charge of murdering his ex-wife and a friend. Then the friend's father sued Simpson in civil court and won a monetary judgement. This kind of monetary payment is a lot like blood money.
In both systems, there is a separate mechanism for handling cases which the criminal court system cannot.
 
“Poetry” shows us a gentle, intelligent, working-class woman who is deeply shamed. We see her in the context of a Korean society that forces difficult decisions on her. She silently keeps her own moral compass expressing herself only at the end with a poem.
“Poetry” provides a nonWestern way of looking at life. Get into its mindset and you will find the ending bittersweet.
J Adrian Zimmer
