The Schröder/Bernstein Theorem

Using our terminology the Schröder/Bernstein theorem states:
If two sets are size-bounded by each other then they are size-equal.
To show two, potentially uncountable, sets are size-equal, the proof constructs the necessary complete matching using the concept of a link chain. The second section of this chapter defines link chains. The third section derives some properties of link chains. The fourth contains the actual proof.
The first and last sections are not directly related to the proof. The first section reviews some properties of sets and the last section provides two examples of how the construction described in the proof works on actual sets.

A more obvious principle that is used without comment is that if B is defined as A-C, then as shown in this diagram A=B⋃C
 
A final simple principle that is used without comment is that if you you know A=B then you also know A⋂C=B⋂C. This works when A is e(M) and A⋂C is e(M,C).
 
Unions and intersections involving e may not work quite the way you would expect. For relations M and M'
  • e(M⋃M')=e(M)⋃e(M') because both consist of all the ends or links in M and M'
  • e(M⋂M') is not necessarily e(M)⋂e(M') because there might be links {x,y} in M and {x,z} in M' with y≠z. In that case it is possible for e(M⋂M') to be empty but e(M)⋂e(M') to contain x.

Abstract Tool: Link Chains

Link chains are sequences of links from two matchings. They are not the same as the subset chains of Chapter 8. In this chapter and in many places they are simply called chains.
To define a chain we need two matchings R and Q between two sets A and B such that R covers A (i.e. e(R,A)=A) and Q covers B (i.e. e(Q,B)=B).
In this diagram I have made two copies of the same A and B to help differentiate between R and Q.
The chains which interest us begin in B-e(R,B) like this
Define B* to be B-e(R,B) and a B*-chain to be a chain of links from R⋃Q like this
{c0,c1},{c1,c2},{c2,c3},…
where c0∊B*. In this section we will see that B*-chains exist exactly when B*≠empty and what their properties are.
P10.1
A potential B*-chain begins with a c0 which is in B* and continues with a link {c0,c1} which is in Q and whose other end c1 is in A. Moreover c0 is not in any other link of R⋃Q.
Proof
Can you prove this?
 

answer

By definition of a B*-chain c0∊B*. By definition of B*, c0 cannot be in any link of R. Since Q covers B, c0 is in a link {c0,c1} of Q. Since Q separates B, c0 cannot be in any other link of Q. Since Q is a relation between A and B, c1 must be in A.
P10.2
For any c∊B* a B*-chain exists and has these properties:
  1. For any odd i∊N:
    ci is in A and in exactly two links of R⋃Q, {ci-1,ci}∊Q and {ci,ci+1}∊R
  2. For any even i∊N:
    ci is in B and in exactly two links of R⋃Q, {ci-1,ci}∊R and {ci,ci+1}∊Q

Proof

Since I think of this chain as being constructed, link by link, from {c0,c1}, an inductive proof best fits my intuition. In the base case, i=1 and so P10.1 shows that {ci-1,ci}∊Q and c1∊A. Since R covers A, there is a {ci,ci+1}∊R. Since R and Q separate A, there can be no other links in R⋃Q which contain ci. Thus P10.2.1 is satisfied for i=1.

For the inductive step assume for an arbitrary natural number i>1, that
  1. For any odd j∊N with j<i:
    cj is in A and in exactly two links of R⋃Q, {cj-1,cj}∊Q and {cj,cj+1}∊R
  2. For any even j∊N with j<i:
    cj is in B and in exactly two links of R⋃Q, {cj-1,cj}∊R and {cj,cj+1}∊Q
Then for an odd natural number greater than 1:
i-1 is even and (taking j=i-1) satisfies the second inductive assumption). Therefore ci-1∊B and {ci-1,ci}∊Q so ci must be in A. Since R covers A, there is a ci+1∊B with {ci,ci+1}∊R. Since R and Q separate A, there can be no other links in R⋃Q which contain ci. Thus P10.2.1 is satisfied.
And for an even natural number greater than 1:
i-1 is odd and (taking j=i-1) satisfies the first inductive assumption). Therefore ci-1∊A and {ci-1,ci}∊R so ci must be in B. Since Q covers B, there is a ci+1∊A with {ci,ci+1}∊Q. Since R and Q separate B, there can be no other links in R⋃Q which contain ci. Thus P10.2.2 is satisfied.
It is clear from P10.1 and P10.2 that given any c∊B* there is exactly one B*-chain that starts with c. Call it Cc.
 
P10.3
  • A B*-chain, starting at c∊B*, along with its sequence or R links and its sequence of Q links, looks like this
    Cc = {c0,c1},{c1,c2},{c2,c3},… R links: {c1,c2},{c3,c4},{c5,c6},… Q links: {c0,c1},{c2,c3},{c4,c5},…
  • Every element of A⋃B that is in e(Cc) is an end of some Q link.
  • Except for c=c0, every element of A⋃B that is in e(Cc) is an end of some R link.
  • Every link of R⋃Q that has an end in e(Cc) is a link of Cc and therefore has both ends in e(Cc).
Proof
This follows from P10.1 and P10.2.
 
The basic idea behind the following proof of the Schröder/Bernstein Theorem is: if we alter R by replacing the R links in all the B*-chains with Q links, then B* becomes empty and so we will have altered R to make sure that it covers A and B. That we can do this and still have a matching may seem obvious but there are some details to consider.

Multiple B*-chains

Let
UC = ⋃{e(Cc)|c∊B*}
 
P10.4
If an end of a link lk of R⋃Q is in UC then both ends of lk are in the same B*-chain.
Proof
Suppose lk has an end in UC, then there is a c such that an end of lk is in Cc. But P10.3 tells us because lk has an end in e(Cc), lk is itself a link of Cc and has its other end in e(Cc) as well.
 
Let
L2 be all links in R⋃Q with two ends in UC
 
L0 be all elements of R⋃Q with no ends in UC
 
P10.5
  • L2 is the set of all links of R⋃Q with at least one end in UC
  • e(L2) = e(UC)
Proof
The first claim follows from P10.4.

Note e(UC) is the set of all ends of links in R⋃Q that have at least on end in UC, but by the first claim this is exactly what e(L2) is.
 
P10.6
  • e(L2)⋃e(L0)=A⋃B
  • e(L2)⋂e(L0)=∅
Proof
Can you prove this??
 
answer
From P10.5 and the definition of L0, L2⋃L0=R⋃Q. But e(R⋃Q)=A⋃B because R covers A and Q covers B. Therefore e(L2)⋃e(L0)=A⋃B.

Note e(L2) is the set of all ends of links with both ends in a B*-chain and e(L0) is the set of all ends of links with no ends in a B*-chain. Therefore e(L2)⋂e(L0)=∅.

Proof of Schröder/Bernstein

Again we start with two matchings R and Q between sets A and B, where R covers A and Q covers B.
Define B*, Cc, UC, L2 and L1 as in the previous sections. If B*=∅, then B=e(R,B) and so R covers both A and B. In that case we already know A is size-equal to B as required.
So the proof considers the case where B*≠∅. It works by using P9.1 and a diagram similar to this
to construct a complete matching between A and B.
Since L2⋂Q and L0⋂R are relations between A and B, P7.1 and P8.1 tell us
  • L2⋂Q is a complete matching between e(L2⋂Q,A) and e(L2⋂Q,B).
  • L0⋂R is a complete matching between e(L0⋂R,A) and e(L0⋂R,B).
However we also need to know that A and B are appropriately partitioned. This will follow when we have shown that
e(L2⋂Q,A) = e(L2,A) e(L0⋂R,A) = e(L0,A) e(L2⋂Q,B) = e(L2,B) e(L0⋂R,B) = e(L0,B)
Let's see why the equalities hold true.
Regarding L2
e(L2⋂Q,A) = e(L2,A) e(L2⋂Q,B) = e(L2,B)
This is true because:
P10.3 tells us that any element of a B*-chain that is covered by a link of R is covered by a link of Q. That means removing R links from L2 does not affect e(L2) so e(L2⋂Q)=e(L2). The desired results follow from intersecting this equation with A and then B on both sides.
Regarding L0
e(L0⋂R,A) = e(L0,A) e(L0⋂R,B) = e(L0,B)
This is true because:
  • Considering elements of A: R covers A so anything covered by Q in A is also covered by R. That means removing links of Q from L0 does not affect e(L0,A).
  • Considering elements of B: from the definition of B*, B=B*⋃e(R,B). So
    e(L0⋂R,B) = e(L0⋂R) ⋂ (B*⋃e(R,B))
    but we are only looking at links in R and none of them has an end in B*, so what we need to show is e(L0⋂R,e(R,B))=e(L0). This is easy. Since R covers e(R,B), anything covered by Q in e(R,B) is also covered by R. That means removing links of Q from L0 does not affect e(L0,B).
Therefore
  • L2⋂Q is a complete matching between e(L2,A) and e(L2,B).
  • L0⋂R is a complete matching between e(L0,A) and e(L0,B).
and the construction we are applying P9.1 to is
Intersecting both sides of the equations in P10.6 with A and B tells us that {e(L2,A),e(L0,A)} partitions A and {e(L2,B),e(L0,B)} partitions B.
The pre-conditions being satisfied, P9.1 tells us that a complete matching between A and B can be constructed, and so the theorem is proved.
